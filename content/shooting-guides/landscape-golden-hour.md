# Landscape Photography During Golden Hour

Golden hour is the period shortly after sunrise and before sunset when the sun sits low on the horizon, casting warm, directional light that transforms ordinary landscapes into extraordinary images. The soft, angled light creates long shadows that reveal texture and depth, while the warm color temperature adds richness to every scene. Mastering golden hour photography is one of the most impactful skills a landscape photographer can develop.

## What You Need

- **Camera**: Any interchangeable lens camera (mirrorless or DSLR) with manual exposure controls
- **Lenses**: A wide-angle zoom (16-35mm) for sweeping vistas and a mid-range zoom (24-70mm) for tighter compositions
- **Tripod**: A sturdy tripod rated for your camera and lens weight, essential for sharp images at smaller apertures
- **Filters**: A circular polarizer (CPL) to deepen skies and cut glare, and a 2-3 stop graduated neutral density (GND) filter to balance bright skies with darker foregrounds
- **Remote shutter release**: Wired or wireless, to eliminate camera shake during longer exposures
- **Headlamp**: For navigating in pre-dawn darkness or packing up after sunset

## Camera Settings

- **Aperture**: f/8 to f/16 for maximum depth of field. f/11 is the sweet spot for most lenses, balancing sharpness with diffraction.
- **Shutter Speed**: Varies with aperture and ISO. Typically 1/30 to 1 second on a tripod. Use mirror lock-up or electronic first curtain shutter to minimize vibration.
- **ISO**: 100-400. Keep ISO as low as possible for maximum dynamic range and minimum noise. Base ISO (typically 100) is preferred.
- **White Balance**: Set to Daylight (5200K) or Shade (7500K) to preserve and enhance the warm tones. Alternatively, shoot RAW and adjust in post.
- **Focus**: Manual focus using live view magnification, focused at the hyperfocal distance for your focal length and aperture.
- **Metering**: Evaluative/matrix metering as a starting point, then adjust with exposure compensation. Bracket +/- 1-2 stops for high-contrast scenes.

## Technique Tips

1. **Arrive early and stay late.** The best light often occurs 10-20 minutes before sunrise and after sunset, during the transition between blue hour and golden hour. Scout your composition in advance so you are ready when the light peaks.

2. **Use foreground interest.** Wide-angle golden hour landscapes are strongest when they include compelling foreground elements like rocks, wildflowers, or water. Get low and close to your foreground subject to exaggerate its presence in the frame.

3. **Shoot into the light.** While side-lighting reveals texture, shooting toward the sun during golden hour creates dramatic backlit scenes with warm flare and glowing edges. Use a lens hood and be deliberate about flare placement.

4. **Work the scene quickly.** Golden hour light changes rapidly. Have your composition planned before the light arrives, and shoot continuously through the transition. Move through 3-4 pre-scouted compositions rather than spending all your time on one.

5. **Bracket exposures for HDR or blending.** The dynamic range between a bright golden sky and a shadowed foreground often exceeds what a single exposure can capture. Shoot 3-5 brackets at 1-stop intervals and blend in post-processing.

6. **Watch the sky behind you.** While everyone photographs the sunset, the light falling on the landscape opposite the sun can be equally beautiful. Warm, direct light on mountains, buildings, or clouds to the east during sunset can yield stunning results.

## Common Mistakes

- **Arriving too late.** The best golden hour light is fleeting. If you arrive at sunset, you have already missed the peak. Plan to be in position 45-60 minutes before the sun reaches the horizon.
- **Ignoring the foreground.** A dramatic sky over a boring foreground produces a mediocre image. Spend as much time finding foreground interest as you do waiting for good light.
- **Over-saturating in post.** Golden hour images are naturally warm and colorful. Heavy-handed saturation and vibrance adjustments look unnatural. Use targeted adjustments rather than global sliders.
- **Forgetting to check focus.** In the rush of changing light, it is easy to forget to verify critical focus. Zoom to 100% in live view after focusing, and check your images on the LCD periodically.
