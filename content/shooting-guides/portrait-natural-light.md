# Portrait Photography with Natural Light

Natural light portrait photography is the most accessible way to create beautiful, flattering images of people. By learning to read, find, and shape available light, you can produce professional-quality portraits without any artificial lighting equipment. The key is understanding how the direction, quality, and color of natural light affect your subject, and positioning them accordingly.

## What You Need

- **Camera**: Any interchangeable lens camera with aperture priority or manual mode.
- **Lenses**: An 85mm f/1.8 is the classic portrait lens, offering flattering compression and beautiful background blur. A 50mm f/1.8 is an affordable alternative. A 35mm f/1.4 works for environmental portraits with more context.
- **Reflector**: A 5-in-1 collapsible reflector (silver, gold, white, black, translucent) fills shadows and adds catch lights. The white and silver sides are most commonly used.
- **Diffuser**: The translucent panel from a 5-in-1 reflector can soften harsh sunlight when held between the sun and your subject.
- **Lens hood**: Prevents flare when shooting in backlit conditions.

## Camera Settings

- **Aperture**: f/1.8 to f/4 for individual portraits with blurred backgrounds. f/1.8-2.8 produces the creamiest bokeh. f/4-5.6 for small groups to ensure everyone is in focus.
- **Shutter Speed**: 1/200 or faster to prevent motion blur. A minimum of 1/focal length (e.g., 1/85 for an 85mm lens). In low light, do not go below 1/125 for portraits.
- **ISO**: 100-800 depending on available light. Keep as low as conditions allow for the cleanest skin tones.
- **Focus**: Single-point AF locked on the near eye. If your camera has eye-detection AF, enable it. The eyes must be tack sharp in portraits.
- **Metering**: Spot metering on the subject's face, or evaluative metering with +1/3 to +2/3 exposure compensation to keep skin tones bright and clean.
- **White Balance**: Shade (7500K) for warm skin tones in open shade, or Daylight (5200K) in direct sunlight. Shoot RAW to fine-tune later.

## Technique Tips

1. **Open shade is your best friend.** Position your subject just inside the edge of shade from a building, tree, or awning. They receive soft, even light from the open sky while being shielded from harsh direct sun. Look for catch lights in the eyes to confirm good light direction.

2. **Face your subject toward the light.** Turn their body so the main light source (the sky, a window, a reflective wall) illuminates their face evenly or creates gentle shadows. A slight angle creates natural dimension without harsh shadows.

3. **Use golden hour for warm, flattering light.** The 30-60 minutes before sunset produces the most universally flattering portrait light. Backlight your subject for a warm rim light and glowing hair, then use a reflector to bounce fill light onto their face.

4. **Watch the background.** A great portrait requires a clean background. Scan the edges of your frame for distracting elements like signs, bright spots, or poles that appear to grow from your subject's head. Move your position or adjust angle to eliminate distractions.

5. **Create connection through direction.** Give your subject something to do or think about. A genuine laugh, a thoughtful gaze, or an interaction with their environment produces more compelling images than a stiff pose. Talk to them, tell jokes, and keep the energy comfortable.

6. **Use window light indoors.** A large window provides beautiful, directional light similar to a studio softbox. Position your subject at a 45-degree angle to the window for classic portrait lighting with gentle shadows that define facial structure.

## Common Mistakes

- **Shooting in direct midday sun.** Harsh overhead light creates dark eye socket shadows (raccoon eyes) and squinting. Move to shade or wait for softer light conditions.
- **Focusing on the wrong eye.** In angled portraits, always focus on the eye closest to the camera. An out-of-focus near eye is immediately noticeable and ruins an otherwise good image.
- **Cluttered backgrounds.** Beginners focus entirely on the subject and ignore everything behind them. Always check your background before pressing the shutter.
- **Underexposing skin tones.** Camera meters often underexpose portraits, especially with backlit subjects. Add +1/3 to +1 stop of exposure compensation to keep skin looking bright and healthy. Check your histogram to ensure highlights are not clipped.
