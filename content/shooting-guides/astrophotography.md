# Astrophotography: Milky Way & Night Sky

Astrophotography reveals the night sky in ways invisible to the naked eye, capturing the luminous band of the Milky Way, star trails, and deep-sky objects with long exposures and high ISOs. It demands careful planning around moon phases, weather, and light pollution, as well as precise camera settings to capture sharp stars without trailing. The results are among the most awe-inspiring images in photography, transforming an ordinary landscape into a window on the cosmos.

## What You Need

- **Camera**: A full-frame mirrorless or DSLR with strong high-ISO performance (clean images at ISO 3200-6400). APS-C sensors work but with more noise.
- **Lenses**: The fastest wide-angle lens you can afford. f/1.4-2.8 primes in the 14-24mm range are ideal. A 14mm f/2.8, 20mm f/1.8, or 24mm f/1.4 are popular choices.
- **Tripod**: A rock-solid tripod is non-negotiable. Any movement during a 15-25 second exposure will ruin the image.
- **Star tracker** (optional): A motorized equatorial mount like the iOptron SkyGuider Pro allows exposures of several minutes without star trails, dramatically reducing noise.
- **Headlamp with red light mode**: Preserves your night vision while allowing you to see camera controls and navigate terrain.
- **Intervalometer**: For star trail composites and automated time-lapse sequences.
- **Planning apps**: PhotoPills, Stellarium, or Sky Guide for planning Milky Way position, moon phase, and dark sky conditions.

## Camera Settings

- **Aperture**: Wide open (f/1.4 to f/2.8). Every fraction of a stop matters when photons are scarce. If your lens is soft wide open, stop down no more than one-third stop.
- **Shutter Speed**: Use the 500 Rule (500 / focal length = max seconds) as a starting point to avoid star trailing. For a 20mm lens: 500/20 = 25 seconds. For higher-resolution sensors, use the NPF Rule or a stricter 300 Rule.
- **ISO**: 3200-6400 for most modern full-frame cameras. Test your camera in advance to find the highest usable ISO. Some cameras benefit from ISO invariance, where a lower ISO pushed in post produces equivalent results.
- **White Balance**: Set to approximately 3800-4200K for a natural night sky color. Shooting RAW allows adjustment later.
- **Focus**: Manual focus at infinity. Use live view at maximum magnification on a bright star to fine-tune focus. Focus peaking can assist but is not always reliable in low light.
- **Long Exposure Noise Reduction**: Turn OFF. It doubles your exposure time by taking a dark frame after each shot. Instead, shoot dark frames manually at the end of your session for stacking.

## Technique Tips

1. **Plan around the moon.** A bright moon washes out the Milky Way and faint stars. Schedule shoots within 5 days of the new moon, or shoot before moonrise or after moonset. PhotoPills and The Photographer's Ephemeris show exact moon times.

2. **Find truly dark skies.** Use light pollution maps (lightpollutionmap.info) to find Bortle Class 1-4 locations. Even a small city 50 miles away can create a noticeable glow on the horizon. The Milky Way core is invisible from Bortle 7+ urban areas.

3. **Nail focus before it gets dark.** Arrive at your location before sunset and focus on a distant object at infinity using autofocus, then switch to manual focus and tape the focus ring in place. Alternatively, use live view magnification on a bright star after dark.

4. **Shoot multiple exposures for stacking.** Take 10-20 identical frames of the same composition. Stacking them in software like Sequator or DeepSkyStacker averages out random noise, producing a dramatically cleaner final image than a single exposure.

5. **Include a foreground element.** The most compelling astrophotography images combine a stunning sky with an interesting foreground: a mountain, tree, rock formation, or building. Light-paint the foreground with a dim flashlight during exposure or blend a separate blue-hour foreground exposure.

6. **Dress warmer than you think necessary.** Standing still in the dark for hours is far colder than hiking. Bring layers, hand warmers, and a thermos. Cold fingers make camera operation difficult and miserable.

## Common Mistakes

- **Star trails from too-long exposures.** Pixel-level star trailing is the most common beginner mistake. Zoom to 100% on your LCD to verify sharpness. If stars are streaking, shorten your exposure time.
- **Focusing at the hard infinity stop.** Most lenses focus slightly past infinity. The hard stop on the focus ring is NOT correct infinity focus. Always verify visually using live view magnification on a star.
- **Shooting during a bright moon.** Checking moon phase is the single most important planning step. A 75%+ illuminated moon above the horizon will ruin your Milky Way shots.
- **Ignoring light pollution direction.** Even from a dark site, the horizon glow from distant cities will appear in your images. Position the brightest light pollution behind you or use it creatively as a warm glow on the horizon.
