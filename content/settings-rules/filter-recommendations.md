# Filter Recommendations by Scenario

Lens filters remain essential tools in digital photography, solving problems that cannot be fully replicated in post-processing. This guide covers the three most important filter types for landscape and outdoor photography: Graduated Neutral Density (GND), Neutral Density (ND), and Circular Polarizer (CPL), with specific recommendations for when and how to use each.

## Circular Polarizer (CPL)

A CPL reduces reflections, deepens blue skies, and increases color saturation by filtering polarized light. Its effect cannot be replicated in post-processing.

### When to Use

| Scenario | Effect | Recommendation |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| Blue sky with white clouds | Deepens sky, increases cloud contrast | Maximum effect at 90 degrees to the sun |
| Foliage and vegetation | Removes waxy leaf glare, saturates greens | Rotate for maximum saturation |
| Water surface (lakes, streams) | Cuts surface reflections, reveals underwater detail | Adjust rotation to taste; partial reflection can add interest |
| Wet rocks and waterfalls | Removes glare from wet surfaces | Strong polarization for maximum color |
| Automotive/glass photography | Eliminates window reflections | Essential for seeing through glass |
| Rainbow photography | Do NOT use | CPL can eliminate the rainbow |

### CPL Technical Notes

- Reduces exposure by approximately 1-2 stops (varies by filter and rotation angle).
- On ultra-wide lenses (below 24mm full frame), polarization is uneven across the frame, creating a dark band in the sky. Avoid full polarization on wide angles.
- Always buy the highest quality CPL you can afford. Cheap polarizers introduce color casts and reduce sharpness.

## Graduated Neutral Density (GND)

GND filters are half-dark, half-clear, used to balance the brightness between a bright sky and darker foreground in a single exposure.

### GND Types

| Type | Transition | Best For |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Hard-edge GND | Abrupt transition | Flat, unbroken horizons (ocean, plains) |
| Soft-edge GND | Gradual transition | Uneven horizons (mountains, trees, buildings) |
| Reverse GND | Strongest density at center, fading toward top | Sunrise/sunset when brightest light is at the horizon |

### GND Strength Recommendations

| Scenario | GND Strength | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Bright sky, shaded foreground (sunset) | 3-stop (0.9 ND) | Most common general-purpose strength |
| Sunrise/sunset at horizon | 3-stop reverse GND | Darkest at the horizon where the sun meets the land |
| Moderate sky/ground contrast (overcast) | 2-stop (0.6 ND) | Gentle balancing for softer light |
| Extreme contrast (backlit sunset) | 4-stop (1.2 ND) | Rare but useful for very high-contrast scenes |
| Twilight/blue hour | None needed | Sky and ground brightness naturally balance |

### GND Tips

- Rectangular slot-in filters (Cokin, Lee, NiSi) are far more versatile than screw-on GNDs because you can position the transition line anywhere in the frame.
- In situations with complex horizons (trees, buildings breaking the skyline), exposure bracketing and luminosity mask blending in post often produces better results than physical GND filters.

## Neutral Density (ND)

ND filters uniformly reduce light entering the lens, enabling longer shutter speeds for creative motion blur effects in bright conditions.

### ND Strength Guide

| Filter | Stop Reduction | Shutter Speed Effect (from 1/250) | Primary Use |
|--------|---------------|-----------------------------------|-------------|
| ND4 (0.6) | 2 stops | 1/60 | Slight motion blur, wider aperture in bright light |
| ND8 (0.9) | 3 stops | 1/30 | Moderate water blur, shallow DOF portraits outdoors |
| ND64 (1.8) | 6 stops | 1/4 | Smooth waterfalls and streams in daylight |
| ND1000 (3.0) | 10 stops | 4 seconds | Silky water, cloud streaks, removing crowds |
| ND32000 (4.5) | 15 stops | 2 minutes | Extreme long exposure, ghosting moving objects |

### ND Scenario Recommendations

| Scenario | Recommended ND | Settings | Result |
|----------|---------------|----------|--------|
| Waterfall in shade | 3-stop | f/11, 1/4 sec | Silky water, sharp rocks |
| Waterfall in bright sun | 6-stop | f/11, 2 sec | Smooth flowing water |
| Seascape, wave motion | 10-stop | f/11, 30 sec | Misty, ethereal water |
| Cloud movement | 10-stop | f/11, 60-120 sec | Dramatic cloud streaks |
| Crowd removal in daylight | 10-stop | f/16, 30-60 sec | Moving people disappear |
| Shallow DOF portrait in sun | 3-stop | f/1.4, 1/1000 | Wide aperture without overexposure |

### ND Quality Considerations

| Factor | Budget Filters | Premium Filters |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Color cast | Warm or magenta shift common | Color neutral |
| Sharpness | Slight softening | No measurable impact |
| Coating | Prone to flare and ghosting | Multi-coated, flare resistant |
| Recommended brands | — | NiSi, Breakthrough Photography, B+W, Lee |

## Filter Stacking

Filters can be combined for compound effects, but stacking introduces potential issues:

| Combination | Common Use | Watch For |
|------------|-----------|-----------|
| CPL + GND | Polarized sky with balanced exposure | Vignetting on wide-angle lenses |
| CPL + ND | Long exposure with reduced reflections | Color cast accumulation; use slim-profile filters |
| GND + ND | Long exposure with balanced sky | Increased flare risk; use lens hood |
| CPL + GND + ND | All effects combined | Maximum vignetting risk; avoid on lenses wider than 24mm |

## General Recommendations

- **Buy fewer, better filters.** A high-quality 6-stop ND and a premium CPL cover 90% of situations.
- **Match filter size to your largest lens.** Buy filters for your largest filter thread diameter and use step-up rings for smaller lenses.
- **Clean filters frequently.** Fingerprints, water spots, and dust on filters degrade image quality more than having no filter at all.
- **Remove filters when not needed.** Every additional glass element between your lens and subject reduces contrast and introduces potential flare. Use filters intentionally, not as permanent lens protection.
