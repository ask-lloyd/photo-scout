# Hyperfocal Distance Reference

The hyperfocal distance is the focus distance that maximizes depth of field for a given focal length and aperture. When you focus at the hyperfocal distance, everything from half that distance to infinity will be acceptably sharp. This is the foundation of landscape photography focus technique, ensuring front-to-back sharpness in a single exposure.

## How to Use These Tables

1. Find your focal length and aperture in the table below.
2. Focus your lens at the listed hyperfocal distance.
3. Everything from half that distance to infinity will be in acceptable focus.

**Example**: With a 24mm lens at f/11, the hyperfocal distance is 1.7m (5.6 ft). Focus at 1.7m, and everything from 0.85m (2.8 ft) to infinity is sharp.

## Practical Focus Technique

### Method 1: Live View Magnification
1. Set your aperture and focal length.
2. Look up the hyperfocal distance in the table above.
3. Focus on an object at approximately that distance using live view at maximum magnification.
4. Switch to manual focus to lock the setting.

### Method 2: Focus on the Distant Third
When you cannot precisely measure the hyperfocal distance, a reliable rule of thumb is to focus approximately one-third of the way into your scene. This approximates the hyperfocal distance in many typical landscape compositions.

### Method 3: Depth of Field Preview
Use your camera's depth of field preview button (if available) at your working aperture to visually confirm that both foreground and background appear sharp through the viewfinder.

## When NOT to Use Hyperfocal Distance

- **Astrophotography**: Focus at true infinity for pinpoint stars.
- **Telephoto landscapes**: With long focal lengths, hyperfocal distances become very large. Consider focus stacking instead.
- **Foreground very close to lens**: If your nearest subject is within inches of the lens, focus stacking multiple exposures is more reliable than hyperfocal focusing.
