# Epic Sunset

## What Creates This Opportunity

The most photogenic sunsets occur when broken cloud cover between 30–75% combines with high-altitude cirrus clouds. The cirrus acts as a canvas — thin ice crystals at altitude scatter long-wavelength light, producing vivid oranges, magentas, and reds. A completely clear sky yields a pleasant but one-dimensional gradient; too much cloud blocks the light entirely. The sweet spot is partial cover with visible structure.

Moderate humidity (30–80%) keeps enough moisture in the atmosphere to enhance Rayleigh scattering without creating haze that kills contrast. Good visibility (>10 km) ensures the horizon remains clean and the sun's last rays travel unobstructed through the lower atmosphere.

## What to Look For

- **High clouds lighting up 15–30 minutes before official sunset** — this is your signal
- **Gaps in lower cloud layers** that let direct light hit the upper deck
- **A clear western horizon** even if clouds sit overhead; the sun needs a slot to shine through
- **Color progression**: yellow → orange → magenta → purple over 20–40 minutes

## Sample Shots

- Silhouettes of ridgelines or trees against the blazing sky
- Reflections on calm water doubling the color palette
- Wide-angle panoramas capturing the full cloud canvas
- Telephoto compression of layered cloud bands near the horizon

## Tips

- Arrive at least 30 minutes before sunset to scout compositions
- Keep shooting after the sun dips below the horizon — the best color often peaks 5–15 minutes post-sunset
- Use spot metering on the brightest part of the sky to preserve highlight detail
- Bracket exposures; dynamic range can exceed 10 stops between foreground and sky
