# Milky Way Window

## What Creates This Opportunity

Astrophotography of the Milky Way demands the darkest, clearest skies possible. Cloud cover must be very low (<15%) to provide an unobstructed view of the galactic core. Low humidity (<60%) reduces atmospheric moisture that scatters artificial light pollution and dims faint stars. Excellent visibility (>20 km) indicates clean, particle-free air.

Moderate wind is acceptable, but very high wind (>15 km/h) causes vibration on tripod-mounted setups and can carry moisture or dust that degrades transparency.

**Important:** This rule does not account for moon phase or moonrise/moonset times. A bright moon will wash out the Milky Way even under otherwise perfect conditions. Always cross-reference with a moon calendar — target nights within ±5 days of the new moon, or shoot when the moon is below the horizon.

## What to Look For

- **Naked-eye Milky Way visibility** — if you can see the band with your eyes, conditions are excellent
- **Transparent skies** with stars visible all the way to the horizon
- **No high cirrus** that might be invisible by day but scatter light at night
- **The galactic core position** — in the Northern Hemisphere, the core is best visible from April through September, rising in the southeast

## Sample Shots

- The Milky Way arching over a landscape (tracked or untracked)
- Star trails using long cumulative exposures
- Foreground-lit compositions using brief light painting
- Astro panoramas stitched from multiple frames

## Tips

- Use the 500 Rule (500 ÷ focal length = max exposure seconds) to avoid star trails on a static tripod
- Shoot at the widest aperture your lens allows (f/2.8 or faster is ideal)
- ISO 3200–6400 is typical; test your camera's noise performance beforehand
- Focus manually at infinity using live view magnification on a bright star
- Bring a headlamp with a red-light mode to preserve night vision
- Scout and compose during daylight or blue hour so you know exactly where to point in the dark
- Allow 20–30 minutes for your eyes to fully dark-adapt before evaluating sky quality
- Light pollution maps (e.g., Bortle scale) are essential for site selection — aim for Bortle Class 3 or darker
