# Storm Break

## What Creates This Opportunity

A storm break occurs when an active weather system moves through and the trailing edge allows direct sunlight to reach the ground while dramatic clouds still dominate the sky. The key signature is precipitation dropping to near zero while cloud cover remains substantial (40–70%) but broken — gaps open and shafts of intense light spill through.

Post-storm air is washed clean of particulates, boosting visibility and color saturation. Residual humidity keeps clouds textured and three-dimensional. Moderate wind (>10 km/h) ensures the cloud deck keeps moving, creating an ever-changing light show.

## What to Look For

- **A clearing western horizon at sunset** (or eastern at sunrise) with storm clouds still overhead
- **Rain curtains in the distance** contrasting with sunlit foreground
- **Rainbows** — they appear opposite the sun when rain and sunlight coexist
- **Rapidly shifting light** as cloud gaps race across the landscape

## Sample Shots

- A single spotlight of sun illuminating a hillside against dark storm clouds
- Double rainbow arcing over a landscape
- Wet surfaces reflecting warm light (streets, rocks, foliage)
- Dramatic wide-angle skyscapes with towering cumulonimbus

## Tips

- Storm breaks are unpredictable; keep your gear packed and ready to deploy on short notice
- Waterproof your bag and use a rain cover on your camera until conditions stabilize
- The light can change in seconds — pre-visualize compositions so you can shoot fast
- Use a polarizer to manage wet-surface reflections and boost the rainbow if one appears
- These events often last only 10–30 minutes; prioritize your best composition and work it
